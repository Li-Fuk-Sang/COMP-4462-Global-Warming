Global Warming Hong Kong Dashboard

Hong Kong Global Warming Dashbaord

Over the years, global warming had become one of the most talked about issues around our planet. While there are still many disagreements surrounding the severity of warming, empirical evidence has shown that our planet is unarguably warming up when compared to before the industrial revolution. The rise in temperature is hypothesized to lead to irreversible consequences, such as a rise in sea level or extreme weather.

What role is Hong Kong playing in terms of global warming? How is Hong Kong being affected? What have we done to combat our emissions throughout the years? These are essential questions we would need to answer in deciding policies surrounding climate change. This dashboard attempts to answers these questions in a storyline format.

Cause and Effect

An important question to ask when analysing global warming dataset is: whether countries that emits the most suffers the most severe consequences? Short answer: Not necessarily.

The graph to the left plots a country’s CO2 emission against the country’s temperature rise since 1990s. Each country is marked by a bubble, while the size of the bubble encodes the population of the country.

It is easy to see that there are no clear relationships between how much a country emits and how much its temperature increase. In order words, a country might suffer severely from temperature rise even though it is not responsible for making any emissions. This observation leads to some interesting questions. For example, how can we incentivize big countries to make less emissions, while they are often not that ones to be affect by it?

Surprising correlation between mean temperature and temperature rise

Another interesting observation came from the fact that blue dots are generally higher than the orange dots. This implies that colder countries tend to suffer a higher degree of temperature rise when compared to hotter ones. This finding was not hypothesized in the initial proposal and was a surprise to our team.

Where we are

If we change the encoding, we can easily see where Hong Kong is when compared to countries of similar population, which will be important for the discussion to come.

Industrial Sectors

The annual OVERALL Greenhouse Gas (GHG) emission in Hong Kong has been on a steady incline since the millennium. With the growth in population and economy, this should be no surprise. Little did the public know, the government had been secretly plotting for an impact. When the figures for 2020 GHG emissions were released, the tides turned. The reduction of emissions by 25% in 2020 compared to the recent peak in 2014 shocked many, and the secret decarbonisation strategy was a success.

The GHG in Hong Kong can be categorised into six major sectors, “Electricity Generation and Other Energy Industries”, “Transport”, “Waste”, “Industrial Processes and Product Use”, “Agriculture, Forestry and Other Land Use” and “Other End Use of Fuel”. For the last 30 years, the GHG emissions of the first 4 categories have been on an increasing trend, while the remaining 2 declined. Energy generation has always been the largest GHG emitter in Hong Kong, contributing to around two-thirds of the overall emission.

Hong Kong started to improve its electricity generation policy since 2014, gradually replacing coal with natural gas, which is a cleaner fuel. As the two power companies obtained new gas-fired generating units in 2020, the share of natural gas in the fuel mix surged along with the decline in coal. This explains the dramatic drop in GHG emissions by Electricity Generation in 2020, and the all-time low in overall GHG emissions in 19 years.

Another category that has been emitting less GHG in recent years is Transport. Hong Kong has been actively promoting transport electrification, waiving the first registration tax for electric commercial vehicles in full and subsidising trials of over 170 electric goods vehicles under the New Energy Transport Fund. This has resulted in fewer petrol and diesel-propelled vehicles on the road and hence less pollution.

These policies have proved effective and contributed to the significant reduction in GHG emissions in 2020. The government’s long-term commitment to decarbonisation has paid off and Hong Kong is well on its way to a greener future.

Hong Kong's temperature

As shown in the industrial sectors section, Hong Kong's GHG emission has been on a steady incline. GHG emission is known to trap heat which means that Hong Kong's temperature might be on a rise. To visualize this, a symbol map with radial heatmap pop up is made.

Line Chart Symbol Map

A symbol map consisting of line charts is made to show the yearly average temperature of each weather station. By observing the line charts, we can see that Hong Kong's temperature is on a steady increase, confirming the information displayed in the top most visualization. This is also proof that Hong Kong is facing climate change and should execute proper policy to prevent the rising any further.

Radial Heatmap

A comparison should be made at each station to see the impact of climate change on Hong Kong's temperature. This way, the only independent variable is the station's location, and the dependent variable is the temperature change. Since Hong Kong has four seasons, it will only be fair to compare the temperature for each month. Because of this, a radial heatmap is the most suitable data visualization for this task.

By clicking on the line chart, you can find the radial heatmap. The radial heatmap has 12 parts which denote the months of the year. Using a radial heatmap, you can go along the parts and compare temperature change fairly. An exciting finding observed is that utilizing a radial heatmap shows a relatively increasing temperature in all other months on all weather stations. This supports the claims made in the first visualization of Hong Kong’s increasing temperature.

Effect of climate change on Hong Kong's air quality and beach waters

While temperature is a well known factor that is correlated with climate change, there are some other effects that are less known. With greater GHG emissions, air quality in the city is also affected with data showing an increasing amounts of pollutants, such as NO2, O3, and CO2. Meanwhile, with climate change bringing about more frequent storms to the city, flooding and strong rains have caused overflow of sewages to waters of Hong Kong, thus endangering the water quality of water recreational areas such as beachs. Given this, our team has visualized the trend of Hong Kong's air quality index and beach water quality in two visualizations to have a better understanding of how these factors have been affected.

Air Quality Index

A symbol map consisting of line charts is made to show the yearly average air quality index of each weather station. By observing the line charts, we can see that Hong Kong's air quality shows a similar trend in all weather stations. While some line charts indicate worse air quality at certain regions, the overall slowly improving trend is definetly an unexpected finding. With the impact of climate change being felt greater around the world, it is surprising to see a unique trend against expectations. This is proof that Hong Kong is acting on the climate change issue and is executing proper policy to further improve the city's air quality.

Beach Water Quality

A symbol map consisting of markers is made to show the yearly average beach water quality of each beach station in Hong Kong. The interactive visualization allows the user to click on a marker to display the beach name and a line chart of its water quality over time. By observing the line charts, we can see that Hong Kong's beach water quality gradually worsens over time. While there are some cases which indicate beach cleaning or other external factors have returned the beach to low pollution levels, most beaches show significant improvements in quality overtime. This showcases another proof that Hong Kong is acting on the climate change issue and is executing proper policy to also improve the city's beach water quality.
