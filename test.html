Cause and Effect

An important question to ask when analysing global warming dataset is: whether countries that emits the most suffers the most severe consequences? Short answer: Not necessarily.

The graph to the left plots each country’s CO2 emissions (Log Scale) against their rise of temperature from the 1990s. It is easy to see that they have no clear relationship. Indeed, the effect of global warming is not localized, and small countries often suffers disproportionally from the emission of other nations.

Specifically, being able to encode the mean temperature with colour, we are able to observe that colder countries are affected more by temperature rise.

Industrial Sectors

The annual OVERALL Greenhouse Gas (GHG) emission in Hong Kong has been on a steady incline since the millennium. With the growth in population and economy, this should be no surprise. Little did the public know, the government had been secretly plotting for an impact. When the figures for 2020 GHG emissions were released, the tides turned. The reduction of emissions by 25% in 2020 compared to the recent peak in 2014 shocked many, and the secret decarbonisation strategy was a success.

The GHG in Hong Kong can be categorised into six major sectors, “Electricity Generation and Other Energy Industries”, “Transport”, “Waste”, “Industrial Processes and Product Use”, “Agriculture, Forestry and Other Land Use” and “Other End Use of Fuel”. For the last 30 years, the GHG emissions of the first 4 categories have been on an increasing trend, while the remaining 2 declined. Energy generation has always been the largest GHG emitter in Hong Kong, contributing to around two-thirds of the overall emission.

Hong Kong started to improve its electricity generation policy since 2014, gradually replacing coal with natural gas, which is a cleaner fuel. As the two power companies obtained new gas-fired generating units in 2020, the share of natural gas in the fuel mix surged along with the decline in coal. This explains the dramatic drop in GHG emissions by Electricity Generation in 2020, and the all-time low in overall GHG emissions in 19 years.

Another category that has been emitting less GHG in recent years is Transport. Hong Kong has been actively promoting transport electrification, waiving the first registration tax for electric commercial vehicles in full and subsidising trials of over 170 electric goods vehicles under the New Energy Transport Fund. This has resulted in fewer petrol and diesel-propelled vehicles on the road and hence less pollution.

These policies have proved effective and contributed to the significant reduction in GHG emissions in 2020. The government’s long-term commitment to decarbonisation has paid off and Hong Kong is well on its way to a greener future.

Hong Kong's temperature

As shown in the industrial sectors section, Hong Kong's GHG emission has been on a steady incline. GHG emission is known to trap heat which means that Hong Kong's temperature might be on a rise. To visualize this, two visualizations are created: a linear heatmap of 18 weather stations in Hong Kong and a symbol map with radial heatmap pop up.

Effect of climate change on Hong Kong's air quality and beach waters

While temperature is a well known factor that is correlated with climate change, there are some other effects that are less known. With greater GHG emissions, air quality in the city is also affected with data showing an increasing amounts of pollutants, such as NO2, O3, and CO2. Meanwhile, with climate change bringing about more frequent storms to the city, flooding and strong rains have caused overflow of sewages to waters of Hong Kong, thus endangering the water quality of water recreational areas such as beachs. Given this, our team has visualized the trend of Hong Kong's air quality index and beach water quality in two visualizations to have a better understanding of how these factors have been affected.
