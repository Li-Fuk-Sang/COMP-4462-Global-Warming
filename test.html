Cause and Effect

An important question to ask when analysing global warming dataset is: whether countries that emits the most suffers the most severe consequences? Short answer: Not necessarily.

The graph to the left plots each country’s CO2 emissions (Log Scale) against their rise of temperature from the 1990s. It is easy to see that they have no clear relationship. Indeed, the effect of global warming is not localized, and small countries often suffers disproportionally from the emission of other nations.

Specifically, being able to encode the mean temperature with colour, we are able to observe that colder countries are affected more by temperature rise.

Where we are

If we change the encoding, we can easily see where Hong Kong is when compared to countries of similar population, which will be important for the discussion to come.

It is not difficult to see that Hong Kong is moderate in terms of temperature rise.

An other interesting thing we can observe is that although the population of Hong Kong is relatively small, it emits CO2 comparable to the levels of some fellow large countries.

Industrial Sectors

The annual OVERALL Greenhouse Gas (GHG) emission in Hong Kong has been on a steady incline since the millennium. With the growth in population and economy, this should be no surprise. Little did the public know, the government had been secretly plotting for an impact. When the figures for 2020 GHG emissions were released, the tides turned. The reduction of emissions by 25% in 2020 compared to the recent peak in 2014 shocked many, and the secret decarbonisation strategy was a success.

The GHG in Hong Kong can be categorised into six major sectors, “Electricity Generation and Other Energy Industries”, “Transport”, “Waste”, “Industrial Processes and Product Use”, “Agriculture, Forestry and Other Land Use” and “Other End Use of Fuel”. For the last 30 years, the GHG emissions of the first 4 categories have been on an increasing trend, while the remaining 2 declined. Energy generation has always been the largest GHG emitter in Hong Kong, contributing to around two-thirds of the overall emission.
